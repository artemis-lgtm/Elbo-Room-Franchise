# Entity Structure Options & SBA 504 Compatibility

*Last verified: March 25, 2026*
*Status: DRAFT — Requires franchise attorney review before any entity formation*

---

## The Core Problem

The Elbo Room needs to accomplish three things simultaneously, and the entity structure has to support all three:

1. **License the brand** to third-party bar operators
2. **Qualify for SBA 504 financing** to acquire the property
3. **Protect the family** from liability across multiple licensed locations

These objectives create tension. SBA 504 requires owner-occupied property. Licensing creates third-party liability exposure. Family protection requires separation of assets. The entity structure has to thread all three needles.

## Option 1: Single Operating LLC (Simple but Risky)

```
Higney Family
    └── Elbo Room LLC (owns brand + operates bar + holds property)
```

**Pros:**
- Simplest structure
- Clearly SBA 504 eligible (owner-operated, single entity)
- Lowest formation/maintenance cost

**Cons:**
- Zero liability isolation between licensing operations and property
- A lawsuit from a licensed location could theoretically reach the property
- No separation between operating cash flow and licensing revenue
- Franchise attorney will almost certainly reject this

**Verdict: Not recommended.** Too much exposure.

## Option 2: Brand + Operating Split (Common Franchise Structure)

```
Higney Family
    ├── Elbo Room Operations LLC (operates Fort Lauderdale bar)
    │       └── Holds lease → eventually holds property
    └── Elbo Room Brand LLC (owns IP, runs licensing program)
            └── Licensing agreements with third-party operators
```

**Pros:**
- Clean separation of brand liability from property
- Licensing disputes can't touch the real estate
- Standard franchise/licensing structure that attorneys know well
- Each entity can have its own capitalization and credit history

**Cons:**
- SBA 504 complication: which entity buys the property? Operations LLC needs to be the borrower AND occupant
- Brand LLC revenue doesn't directly strengthen Operations LLC's loan application unless there's an intercompany arrangement
- Two entities means two sets of tax filings, registered agents, compliance

**SBA 504 compatibility: HIGH** — Operations LLC is the occupant, Operations LLC borrows. Brand revenue can flow via management fees or distributions.

**Verdict: Strong default option.** Most franchise attorneys will recommend something close to this.

## Option 3: Holding Company Structure (Maximum Protection)

```
Elbo Room Holdings LLC (family-owned parent)
    ├── Elbo Room Fort Lauderdale LLC (operates flagship)
    ├── Elbo Room IP LLC (owns trademarks, brand assets)
    ├── Elbo Room Licensing LLC (manages license program)
    └── Elbo Room Property LLC (holds real estate)
```

**Pros:**
- Maximum liability isolation
- Each business function is firewalled
- Property is protected from ALL operational liability
- Clean for estate planning and future ownership transitions

**Cons:**
- Expensive to form and maintain ($5K-$10K/year in compliance)
- SBA 504 may not qualify Property LLC if it's not the "operator"
- Over-engineered for current scale (0 licensed locations)
- Tax complexity increases significantly

**SBA 504 compatibility: MEDIUM** — Requires careful structuring. The SBA needs to see an owner-occupant, and a passive property-holding LLC may not qualify. Would need the operating LLC to be the borrower with the property LLC as guarantor, or merge the property into the operating entity.

**Verdict: Aspirational.** Right structure for 10+ locations and $15M property purchase, but premature today.

## Option 4: Hybrid (Recommended Starting Point)

```
Higney Family
    ├── Elbo Room LLC (operates bar + holds lease/property)
    └── Elbo Room Heritage LLC (owns IP + manages licensing)
```

**Phase 1 (2026-2028):** Two entities. Operations handles the bar and will be the SBA borrower. Heritage handles IP and licensing. Simple, clean, SBA-compatible.

**Phase 2 (2029+, if licensing scales):** Add Property LLC under a holding company when scale justifies the compliance cost. Restructure before acquisition, not after.

**Pros:**
- SBA 504 eligible (Operations LLC is owner-occupant)
- Liability separation between licensing and property
- Not over-engineered for current scale
- Clear migration path to Option 3 when needed

**Cons:**
- Property and operations are co-mingled in Phase 1 (acceptable risk at current scale)
- Will require restructuring before acquisition (but that's 3-5 years away)

**SBA 504 compatibility: HIGH**

**Verdict: This is the move.** Simple enough to execute now, sophisticated enough to not paint anyone into a corner.

## SBA 504 Qualification Checklist

Regardless of entity structure, the borrowing entity must meet:

- [ ] **Owner-occupied:** Business must occupy 51%+ of the property
- [ ] **For-profit:** Operating business (not passive investment)
- [ ] **Net worth:** Under $15M (shouldn't be an issue)
- [ ] **Net income:** Under $5M average over 2 years (check this)
- [ ] **Job creation/retention:** Must create or retain jobs (bar staff qualifies)
- [ ] **U.S. business:** Operating in the United States
- [ ] **Not a passive business:** Active management required (bar is active)
- [ ] **Creditworthy:** Must demonstrate ability to repay

### SBA 504 + Licensing Revenue Question

**Can licensing revenue from Heritage LLC strengthen the SBA application for Operations LLC?**

Maybe. If Heritage LLC pays a management fee or distribution to Operations LLC (documented, arms-length), that income can appear on Operations LLC's financials. But the SBA lender will scrutinize intercompany transfers. The franchise attorney and an SBA-experienced lender need to structure this together.

**Sable flagged this correctly:** the entity structure conversation IS the franchise attorney's first job, not trademark filing. The SBA compatibility has to be designed in from day one, not retrofitted.

## Critical Dependency: Franchise Attorney

**Nothing in this document should be acted on without franchise counsel.** Entity structure, SBA compatibility, licensing agreement terms, trademark strategy — all of it flows from the attorney's guidance on how Florida franchise law and FTC rules apply to the Heritage License model.

The specific risk: if the Heritage License Program is structured in a way that triggers the FTC Franchise Rule (ongoing control + fee + trademark), the "licensing" model becomes a de facto franchise and requires full FDD compliance ($50K-$75K+ in legal costs). The attorney's first job is drawing the line between licensing and franchising for THIS specific program.

**This is the single most important hire in the entire project. It needs to happen first.**
