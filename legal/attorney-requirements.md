# Franchise Attorney Requirements

_What to look for and what to ask before engaging counsel._

## Why This Is Step One

Everything in the Heritage License Program depends on a franchise attorney's determination of where our model falls on the legal spectrum. The FTC Franchise Rule is not flexible — if our agreement accidentally qualifies as a franchise, we're in violation of federal law from day one. This isn't something to figure out after licenses are sold.

## What We Need From Counsel

1. **FTC Classification:** Does the Heritage License model, as described in `strategy/franchise-vs-licensing.md`, legally constitute a franchise or a license? Where exactly is the line?

2. **Agreement Drafting:** A model licensing agreement that stays definitively on the correct side of the FTC rule while preserving enough brand protection to prevent licensee damage.

3. **State-by-State Analysis:** Franchise registration requirements vary by state. If we're a franchise, we need to register in every franchise registration state before selling. If we're a license, different (lighter) rules apply. Need clarity for at least Florida, and then target expansion states.

4. **Trademark Strategy:** Audit current trademark status of "Elbo Room" and related marks. Advise on any additional filings needed before licensing.

5. **Entity Structure Guidance:** How should the licensing entity be structured relative to the existing bar operations? Key constraint: must preserve SBA 504 loan eligibility for the eventual property purchase.

## What to Look For in Counsel

### Must-Haves:
- **Specific franchise law experience** — Not just a general business attorney who "can handle it." Franchise law is specialized. FTC compliance is not intuitive.
- **Florida bar membership** — The original location is in Florida, and Phase 2 pilot locations are Florida-focused.
- **FDD preparation experience** — Even if we determine we're a license, we need someone who knows the franchise side well enough to ensure we stay clear of it.
- **Restaurant/bar industry experience** — Preferred. The operational realities of bar licensing are different from retail or service franchising.

### Red Flags:
- Attorney who says "we can figure it out as we go" — This is a compliance-first field
- No specific franchise clients in their portfolio
- Unwillingness to give a clear franchise-vs-license determination before drafting agreements
- Fee structure that incentivizes complexity (hourly with no cap on FDD prep)

## Budget Expectations

- **Initial consultation:** $500–$2,000 (some firms offer free franchise consultations)
- **FTC classification opinion letter:** $2,500–$5,000
- **Model license agreement drafting:** $5,000–$15,000
- **Full FDD preparation (if needed):** $15,000–$50,000+
- **Ongoing compliance (annual):** $5,000–$15,000

If we stay on the licensing side, total legal costs for setup are likely $10K–$25K. If we end up needing a full franchise registration, budget $50K–$75K+ for the first year.

## Recommended Search Approach

1. Florida Bar Association franchise law section referrals
2. International Franchise Association (IFA) member directory
3. Ask other bar/restaurant brands in South Florida for referrals
4. Austin's CRE network may have franchise attorneys they've worked with

## Questions for Initial Consultation

1. Based on our Heritage License model overview, does this look like a franchise or a license to you?
2. What changes would you recommend to ensure it stays on the license side?
3. What's the risk if we get it wrong?
4. Do you have experience with bar/restaurant brand licensing specifically?
5. What's your fee structure for this type of engagement?
6. How long from engagement to having a usable licensing agreement?
7. Can you advise on entity structure that preserves SBA 504 eligibility?
